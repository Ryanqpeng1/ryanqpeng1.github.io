##### Study: Severe Outcomes Among Patients with Coronavirus Disease 2019 (COVID-19) — United States, February 12–March 16, 2020
Link: https://stacks.cdc.gov/view/cdc/85951

##### 1. Analyze and question the data that the scientists use.


This study, condeucted shortly after the beginning of the COVID-19 pandemic in the United States, analyzed data collected from hospitals across the US to determine which groups of people would be more susceptible to the disease and which groups had a higher mortality rate. This data was voluntarily reported by each state, and confirmed by state or public health laboratories. However, some data was missing, including including hospital-ization status, ICU admission, death, and age. As such, the reported percentages are estimations based on known information. 

However, there are a few reasons to question this data. The primary reason to do so is the aformentioned missing data: making estimations based on existing data means that a disproportionately small sample size is being used to report the percentages for certain categories: those with missing data. This leads to a higher probability that the results of the data are not indicative of the actual relationship between COVID-19 death/hospitalization rates and age. Furthermore, the fact that the data was reported by each state leads to the possiblity that some states may intentionally report low numbers in order to avoid mandating a quarantine or for other reasons. While this is unlikeley, there is a possibility that some states may do so in order to avoid quarantine. 

##### 2. Analyze their results. Do you trust their findings? Why or why not?

The data from this study suggests that that older adults, particularly those who already had serious underlying health conditions, are at higher risk for severe COVID-19–associated illness and death than younger people. This supports the same relationship that the data from China indicates. I trust these findings because two distinct sample sizes from two groups of people both suggest the same relationship. Although there is possibility for error due to potential problems with the data that I mentioned above, I believe it is unlikely that this margin for error would be significant enough to suggest an incorrect relationship across two distinct groups with signficant sample sizes. Furthermore, this relationship is one that is supported by trends in other diseases: older people and those with underlying health conditions have been shown to generally be at higher risk for illiness or death for most diseases. The relationship found in this study suggests that COVID-19 follows the same trend as most other diseases, which is a reasonable conclusion. For these two reasons, I trust the findings from this study.  


##### 3. How might "publish or perish" have affected this study?

Although it is still possible that "publish or perish" has affected this study to some degree, I believe that it has done so to a lesser extent and for a different reason than most other studies. The primary reason for the "publish or perish" mindset is the belief that there is signficantly less drawback to publishing incorrect information than there is to not publishing anything and losing potential revenue or profits. While this is the case for most studies that have little to no impact on our daily lives, this specific study would have significant repercussions if incorrect knowledge is published. Spreading misinformation about the pandemic could lead to significant deaths due to people underestimating or ignoring COVID-19. This means that the primary reason for "public or perish" is not a likely motivation to publish incorrect information because the profits associated with publishing this study are far less significant to its authors than the importance of publishing correct information about COVID-19. However, "publish or perish" can still affect this study and is not nonexistent: there is a realistic possibility that the reason for missing data is that the authors wanted to publish this study as soon as possible to prevent the spread of misinformation about COVID-19 (this study was published at the beginning of the pandemic), thus causing them to publish the study before recieving more data. So while I believe "public or perish" due to profit or revenue did not affect this study, it is possible that it still affected this study through time. The "perish" in this sense would be publishing the study too late rather than losing out on potential revenue.
